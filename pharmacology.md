## Case A001 — Acute Asthma Bronchospasm

Patient Presentation:
A 19-year-old college student with a history of asthma arrives at urgent care with severe shortness of breath after running. He is wheezing and using accessory muscles to breathe.

Vitals:
HR: 110 bpm  
BP: 128/76  
RR: 28  
SpO2: 90%  
Temp: 98.6°F

Question:
Which medication should be administered immediately?

Options:
A. Albuterol  
B. Amoxicillin  
C. Aspirin  
D. Acetaminophen  

Correct Answer:
Albuterol


### 🔍 Clues

> **Vital Sign Clue:** The patient's SpO2 is 90% and respiratory rate is 28 — their airway is in immediate distress.

> **Mechanism Clue:** Think about what receptor, when activated in the bronchial smooth muscle, causes rapid relaxation and dilation.

> **Clinical Context Clue:** This is a rescue situation — the target drug class acts within minutes and is inhaled directly into the lungs.

Rationale (Correct Drug):
Albuterol is a **short-acting beta-2 adrenergic agonist (SABA)** that relaxes bronchial smooth muscle and rapidly relieves bronchospasm. It is the first-line rescue medication for **acute asthma symptoms**.

Why the Other Options Are Incorrect:

Amoxicillin  
This antibiotic treats bacterial infections. Asthma bronchospasm is not caused by bacteria, so antibiotics are not appropriate.

Aspirin  
Aspirin inhibits COX enzymes and platelet aggregation. It has no bronchodilatory effect and may actually worsen asthma in aspirin-sensitive patients.

Acetaminophen  
Acetaminophen is an analgesic and antipyretic. It does not treat airway constriction or bronchospasm.

## Case A002 — Fever and Pain Control

Patient Presentation:
A 34-year-old patient presents with influenza-like symptoms including fever, headache, and muscle aches.

Vitals:
HR: 92 bpm  
BP: 118/72  
RR: 18  
SpO2: 98%  
Temp: 102.2°F

Question:
Which medication is most appropriate for symptom relief?

Options:
A. Acetaminophen  
B. Amiodarone  
C. Acyclovir  
D. Albuterol  

Correct Answer:
Acetaminophen


### 🔍 Clues

> **Vital Sign Clue:** Temp is 102.2°F and the patient has influenza — no bacterial or viral pathogen needs targeting, just symptoms.

> **Mechanism Clue:** Consider a drug that acts centrally on the COX pathway to reduce fever and pain without anti-inflammatory properties.

> **Clinical Context Clue:** The safest OTC option for flu symptoms in adults — widely used, non-opioid, and not an antibiotic.

Rationale (Correct Drug):
Acetaminophen is a **centrally acting COX inhibitor** that provides **analgesic and antipyretic effects**. It is commonly used for fever and mild to moderate pain without anti-inflammatory effects.

Why the Other Options Are Incorrect:

Amiodarone  
Amiodarone is a class III antiarrhythmic used for life-threatening cardiac dysrhythmias, not fever.

Acyclovir  
Acyclovir treats herpes virus infections. It does not relieve fever or generalized pain symptoms.

Albuterol  
Albuterol is a bronchodilator used for bronchospasm in asthma and COPD, not fever.

Correct Answer:
Amiodarone


### 🔍 Clues

> **Vital Sign Clue:** HR 168 bpm post-MI with BP 86/54 — this is a hemodynamically unstable ventricular tachycardia.

> **Mechanism Clue:** The correct drug prolongs the cardiac action potential by blocking potassium channels — which class does that describe?

> **Clinical Context Clue:** This is the go-to IV antiarrhythmic for ventricular tachycardia or fibrillation in a code or ICU setting.

Question:
Which medication may help stimulate bladder emptying?

B. Atropine  
C. Amiodarone  
D. Aspirin  

Correct Answer:
Bethanechol


### 🔍 Clues

> **Vital Sign Clue:** Surgery commonly causes temporary bladder dysfunction — the detrusor muscle needs stimulation to contract.

> **Mechanism Clue:** Think of the autonomic nervous system — which receptor, when activated, contracts smooth muscle including the bladder?

Aspirin is an antiplatelet NSAID and has no effect on bladder contraction.

## Case C001 — Heart Failure With Cardiac Remodeling

Patient Presentation:
A 63-year-old patient with chronic heart failure presents for medication management. The provider wants to start a drug that will reduce afterload, decrease aldosterone release, and slow cardiac remodeling.

Vitals:
HR: 96 bpm  
BP: 150/92  
RR: 20  
SpO2: 95%  
Temp: 98.4°F

Question:
Which medication is most appropriate?

Options:
A. Celecoxib  
B. Captopril  
C. Codeine  
D. Clonidine  

Correct Answer:
B. Captopril


### 🔍 Clues

> **Vital Sign Clue:** The provider wants to reduce afterload AND prevent cardiac remodeling — not just lower blood pressure.

> **Mechanism Clue:** Blocking the enzyme that converts angiotensin I to angiotensin II reduces vasoconstriction and aldosterone release.

> **Clinical Context Clue:** ACE inhibitors are cornerstone therapy in heart failure — they improve survival and slow disease progression.

Rationale (Correct Drug):
Captopril is an **ACE inhibitor** that blocks conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II. This causes vasodilation, reduces aldosterone release, and helps **prevent cardiac remodeling**, making it effective for heart failure.

Why the Other Options Are Incorrect:

Celecoxib  
A COX-2 NSAID used for pain and inflammation. NSAIDs may actually worsen heart failure due to fluid retention.

Codeine  
An opioid antitussive used for cough or pain, not cardiovascular disease.

Clonidine  
An alpha-2 agonist that lowers blood pressure centrally but does not improve cardiac remodeling in heart failure.
